Why didn't it pop?

Rubber begins to melt around 120 degrees Celsius, while water boils at 100 degrees Celsius. When a flame touches an inflated balloon, the rubber weakens quickly and the balloon pops. However, when the flame touches a balloon filled with water, the balloon doesn't pop. Instead, it blackens because most of the thermal energy is transferred to the water rather than the rubber. The specific heat capacity of a substance is a measure of the amount of heat energy required to raise the temperature of a unit mass of the substance by one degree Celsius. Generally speaking, substances with a higher specific heat can absorb more heat than those with a lower specific heat. For instance, if you compare a balloon filled with water to one filled with air, you'll notice that the former takes longer to pop. This is because water has a higher specific heat than air, which means it can absorb more heat before it reaches its boiling point.
Molar Heat Of Vaporization
The molar heat of vaporization, a crucial concept in energy calculations, refers to the heat required to either vaporize or condense 1.00 mole of a substance at its boiling point, without any temperature change. This value pertains specifically to one mole of substance undergoing vaporization and is unique to each substance. Expressed in kilojoules per mole (kJ/mol), it signifies the energy needed to convert each mole of substance either from liquid to gas (during boiling) or from gas to liquid (during condensation). It's important to recognize that different substances have distinct molar heats of vaporization.

The formula for molar heat of vaporization is:

q = (ΔH_vap) * (mass/molar mass)


Here's the breakdown of the components:

1) q represents the total amount of heat involved.
2) ΔH_vap denotes the molar heat of vaporization, a constant for a given substance.
3) (mass/molar mass) represents the division to determine the number of moles of substance.


For instance, the molar heat of vaporization for water is 40.7 kJ/mol. To calculate the heat of vaporization, simply divide
this value by the molar mass of water (18.015 g/mol). This provides the heat of vaporization in joules per gram (J/g).

Let's consider a practical example:

Given 49.5 g of water being boiled at its boiling point of 100 °C, we use the molar heat equation to find the required energy:

GRESA Method:

- Given:
- Mass of water (gas): 49.5 g
- Boiling point: 100 °C
- Required:
- Energy required to vaporize the water into gas form
- Equation:
- q = (40.7 kJ/mol) * (49.5 g / 18.0 g/mol)
- Solution:
- Calculate the mass of water in moles using the given mass and molar mass of water.
- Multiply the molar heat of vaporization by the number of moles to find the energy required.
- Answer:
- The energy required to vaporize the given mass of water is [insert calculated value] kJ.



For a scenario where 80.1 g of water exists as a gas at 100 °C, and we need to determine the energy needed to turn it into liquid at the same temperature, we apply the same formula.

It's essential to note that the molar heat of vaporization value is utilized at the solid-liquid phase change, irrespective of the direction (boiling or condensing).

To convert the molar heat of vaporization into J/g, divide the given value expressed in joules by the mass of one mole of water. This yields the energy required to vaporize or condense one gram of water.

Consider also the energy needed to boil or condense specific amounts of water, utilizing the heat of vaporization expressed in J/g.

In summary, the molar heat of vaporization plays a pivotal role in understanding the energy dynamics of phase transitions in substances, offering insights into the energy requirements for vaporization and condensation processes.
